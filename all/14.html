  • Critique of Existing Methods: Standard intelligence tests, particularly multiple-choice formats, are flawed. They fail to measure the subject's confidence in their answers and lack financial incentives for truthful responses, capturing only modal beliefs.
  • Proposed Solution: The study introduces a methodology to measure intelligence that incorporates properly incentivized elicitation of subjective confidence.
  • Implementation: This method was applied to the Raven Advanced Progressive Matrices test, a canonical measure of fluid intelligence.
  • Key Finding on Gender and Intelligence: Despite historical data showing men outperforming women on the Raven test, this study finds that women perform significantly better than men when responding to incentivized measures of their confidence.
  • Application to Competitiveness: The results suggest that women do not inherently lack the confidence to compete; rather, they respond to the risks of competition as a rational, risk-averse agent would.
  • Application to Financial Literacy: When confidence is correctly measured, "do not know" responses from women are not evidence of an aversion to answering but a rational expression of their level of certainty.
