  • Thesis: Evaluates the aggregate effects of using tariff revenue for fiscal reform, arguing that the welfare-maximizing tariff is substantially higher when revenue offsets other distortionary taxes compared to a lump-sum redistribution.
  • Model: A multi-country dynamic general equilibrium model integrating:
    • Standard international fiscal policy features (taxes on capital/labor income).
    • Dynamic, heterogeneous-firm trade model with fixed export costs and endogenous trade participation.
    • Roundabout production structure with significant use of imported intermediates, particularly in investment goods.
  • Scenarios Analyzed: A permanent, unilateral US import tariff increase from 4% to 18-20%, with revenue used to:
    1. Rebate lump-sum to households.
    2. Reduce labor income tax.
    3. Reduce capital income tax.
    4. Subsidize investment in physical capital.
  • Key Findings:
    • Using tariff revenue for fiscal reform consistently boosts economic activity more than lump-sum rebates.
    • The welfare-maximizing unilateral tariff is ~18% when revenue subsidizes investment, versus 0% (or negative) with lump-sum rebates. This challenges conventional trade analysis assumptions.
    • Tariff revenue dynamics are crucial: revenue is higher in the short-run before long-run import substitution fully materializes.
    • An investment subsidy is more effective for growth than a capital tax cut because it directly counteracts the tariff-induced price increase on import-heavy investment goods.
    • A temporary 20 p.p. unilateral tariff used for an investment subsidy can increase revenue by ~2% of GDP and temporarily boost the trade balance.

published: D:20250818191815Z
  • Study Objective: To quantify the effectiveness of US workforce development programs (WIOA/WIA) in helping workers from AI-exposed occupations retrain for new jobs, particularly AI-intensive ones.
  • Dataset & Methodology:
    • Analyzes 1.6 million individual training spells from 2012-2023, linked to administrative earnings records.
    • Occupational AI exposure is measured using indices from Brynjolfsson et al. (2018) and Eloundou et al. (2024).
    • Compares trainees to a matched control group (nearest-neighbor from similar AI-exposed occupations) who only received job search assistance.
  • Key Findings:
    • Workers from high AI-exposure occupations see significant earnings returns from training (+$1,470/quarter), though this is 25% lower than returns for low-exposure workers.
    • A penalty exists for specializing in AI: trainees targeting AI-intensive occupations have 29% lower earnings returns than those pursuing more general training.
    • Positive returns are primarily driven by recent years with tight labor markets, suggesting training has higher signal value when firms hire more broadly.
  • Contribution:
    • Introduces an "AI Retrainability Index" (AIR) to rank occupations based on the share of workers who successfully transition to higher-wage, more AI-intensive roles.
    • Estimates that 25-40% of occupations are "AI retrainable" based on the AIR index.
