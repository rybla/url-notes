My Weekly Reading for August 10, 2025 - Econlib
published: 2025-08-10
  • Child Protective Services (CPS) Overreach: A case study where CPS investigated a mother for allowing unsupervised outdoor play, citing internal rules that exceed Virginia state law by requiring parents to be "visible to... neighbors" regardless of whether the children are visible to the parent.
  • Guaranteed Annual Income (GAI) Study: An NBER working paper details a randomized controlled trial where a group receiving $1,000/month for three years saw a reduction in work equivalent to ~8 days/year. While household income increased by ~$6,100, this was driven by the transfer payment itself.
  • Critique of Economic Myths: A review of "The Triumph of Economic Freedom" by Gramm and Boudreaux, which uses historical data to debunk common economic fallacies (e.g., that the New Deal ended the Great Depression or that free trade impoverished America). The book highlights significant global welfare gains from 1980-2000, such as a 43% decline in malnutrition death rates and a six-year increase in global life expectancy, concurrent with population growth.
  • Tariff Uniformity: An economic thought experiment by Alex Tabarrok demonstrates that a uniform tariff on imports from multiple countries (e.g., a 10% tariff on cars from both Japan and Korea) can be welfare-improving compared to a selective tariff on just one country, assuming the goods are perfect substitutes. The uniformity of the tariff is a critical assumption.

The Future of Art with AI - Econlib
published: 2025-08-12
  • Law of Demand: As AI lowers the price of creating art, the quantity of art demanded will increase.
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: The increased demand will primarily serve low-marginal-benefit uses (e.g., personal jokes, blog post illustrations) that were previously not worth the cost of hiring an artist. This creates a new market rather than displacing jobs.
  • Imperfect Substitution: For high-marginal-use art (e.g., commercial products, custom portraits), AI is an imperfect substitute for human artists due to difficulties in communication, interpretation, and achieving specific, nuanced results.
  • Market Bifurcation: The art market is expected to split:
    • A low-marginal-use segment dominated by AI.
    • A high-marginal-use segment where human artists maintain a comparative advantage.
  • Job Impact: Job losses will be concentrated on "intramarginal" artists, whose work is most directly substitutable by AI's capabilities. The overall impact is not predicted to be an existential crisis for the art industry.
