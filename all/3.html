Experiments with Futarchy
published: 2025-09-25

Manifold becomes a battleground for a long-standing debate

🔗
2025-09-26
published: D:20250803121523-04'00'
  • Thesis: Defensive hiring by incumbent firms with monopsony power reduces creative destruction. This helps explain the paradox of rising R&D spending and declining TFP growth in the US.
  • Mechanism: Incumbents hire researchers not just for innovation but to raise the wage costs for potential entrants, thereby stifling competition. This strategy is most effective when the supply of research labor is inelastic.
  • Model: The paper presents a theoretical model based on Schumpeterian creative destruction (Aghion and Howitt, 1992) that balances two forces for an incumbent:
    1. The incentive to use monopsony power to underpay researchers.
    2. The incentive to engage in defensive hiring to reduce the probability of being displaced by an innovator.
  • Conditions: Defensive hiring dominates when "ideas are getting harder to find," which corresponds to:
    • Small innovation size.
    • Low R&D efficiency for entrants.
    • High entry costs.
  • Empirical Evidence:
    • Confirms that the US research labor supply is inelastic.
    • Shows incumbent R&D spending is negatively correlated with creative destruction and sectoral TFP growth.
    • Finds that this strategy extends the lifespan of incumbent firms.
  • Data: Utilizes a novel dataset combining USPTO patent applications (1970-2019) with individual inventor data.
