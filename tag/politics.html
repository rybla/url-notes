*Saving Can-Do* - Marginal REVOLUTION
politicslaweconomicsus politicsgovernancepolicybureaucracyregulationderegulationinfrastructurelaw reformlegal reformadministrative lawenvironmental lawnepajudicial reviewexecutive powergovernment efficiency
  • Problem: The American "can-do" spirit is hindered by bureaucratic and legal obstacles, requiring systemic reform to restore efficient project execution and decision-making.
  • Proposed Solution: A three-pronged approach involving a new legal framework, a new trust-building institution, and a special commission to design the implementation details.
  • Legal Framework Reforms:
    • Centralized Authority: Designate a single lead agency for each infrastructure category, with ultimate approval authority subject to White House oversight. Federal decisions would preempt state/local approvals for nationally significant projects.
    • Goal-Oriented Regulation: Recodify 50 years of accumulated mandates into a set of public goals that allow for balancing and trade-offs, rather than rigid, prescriptive rules.
    • Executive Override: Grant the executive branch interim authority to approve permits "notwithstanding provisions of law to the contrary," provided a public interest justification is given.
    • Streamlined Process: Redefine procedural laws like NEPA to focus on transparency and public comment, with judicial review limited to an "abuse of discretion" standard, not strict procedural compliance.
    • Limited Judicial Review: Curtail court jurisdiction to prevent lawsuits based on procedural inadequacy. Legal challenges should be restricted to actions that exceed the executive's defined authority.

Should Strong Gods Bet On GDP?
politicseconomicssociologycommunityliberalismpolitical philosophystrong godsintentional communitiesfukuyamagdpeconomic growthpost-scarcityuniversal basic incomecultural criticismsocial organization
  • Thesis: Liberalism, as articulated by Fukuyama, is intended as a platform for diverse, voluntary, "strong god" communities to flourish, rather than imposing a single one.
  • Critique: The author argues this model is failing in practice; less than 10% of Americans belong to such intentional communities, with most people defaulting to a mainstream culture they claim to dislike.
  • Hypothesis: The primary inhibitor to forming these communities is economic constraint. Most individuals cannot afford to disengage from the mainstream economy to build or join alternative social structures.
  • Evidence: The author analyzes successful modern communities (e.g., Amish, Ultra-Orthodox Jews, Rationalists), concluding that financial resources (or extreme commitment) are a key factor in their ability to self-organize, co-locate, and fund community projects.
  • Conclusion: Material abundance (high GDP) and strong communities are not opposing but complementary goals. Post-scarcity economics (e.g., UBI after a singularity) could remove the financial barriers that currently prevent people from opting out of mainstream culture and forming their own meaningful communities.

Can AI Mediation Improve Democratic Deliberation?
aidemocracypoliticsdeliberationmediationai mediationlarge language modelsllmscomputational social sciencedeliberative democracyhabermas machineconsensus buildingcommon groundpolitical technologyai ethicsalgorithmic biasfairnessscalabilitysocial choice theorygroup decision-makinghuman-ai collaborationdeliberative trilemma
  • Core Problem: The paper addresses Fishkin's "deliberative trilemma"—the inherent tension between achieving broad participation, political equality, and meaningful deliberation simultaneously in democratic processes.
  • Proposed Solution: The use of AI-mediated deliberation, exemplified by the "Habermas Machine" (HM), an LLM-based system designed to find common ground among diverse viewpoints.
  • Habermas Machine (HM) Architecture:
    • Simulated Election: The HM generates a group statement by simulating an election.
    • Generative Model: Proposes a large set of candidate group statements based on participant inputs.
    • Personalized Reward Model (PRM): Predicts how each participant would rank the candidate statements.
    • Aggregation Mechanism: Uses a social choice function (e.g., Schulze method) to aggregate the predicted rankings and select a winning statement.
    • Iterative Process: Supports multiple rounds of participant critique and AI-driven revision to refine the outcome.
  • Key Findings (from Tessler, Bakker, et al., 2024):
    • The HM outperformed human mediators in generating common ground statements that participants endorsed.
    • The system's scalability and efficiency are its key advantages over human mediation.
    • Iterative rounds of critique and revision led to higher participant approval.
    • The process was found to be fair in representing majority/minority viewpoints, though it could overweight minority critiques in revision rounds.
  • Addressing the Trilemma:
    • Equality (Fairness): Fairness is addressed through architectural design (fair aggregation, ranking, and generation) and requires continuous monitoring with interpretability tools to prevent algorithmic bias.
    • Participation (Scalability): AI mediation can scale to large groups, but this creates a "scalable oversight" problem. Hierarchical aggregation (small groups deliberating, then aggregating group outputs) is proposed as a transparent and verifiable solution.
    • Deliberation (Quality): AI can enhance deliberative quality by acting as an "expert on tap," providing trustworthy information, or serving as a deliberative coach for individuals. This requires careful design to avoid manipulation and ensure AI factuality.
  • Limitations & Future Directions:
    • AI-mediated caucus deliberation lacks the social-relational dynamics (e.g., trust, empathy) of face-to-face interaction and must overcome potential "algorithmic aversion."
    • The system assumes good-faith participation; strategic misrepresentation of preferences is an open research problem.
    • Potential applications range from informal online forums (e.g., X's Community Notes) to formal processes like scaling deliberative polls and facilitating multilateral negotiations.

Fight Chat Control - Protect Digital Privacy in the EU
euchat controldigital privacyprivacysurveillancemass surveillanceencryptionend-to-end encryptionclient-side scanningsecuritycybersecurityfundamental rightsdata protectionlawregulationpolitics
  • Proposal: EU "Chat Control" mandates automated scanning of all private digital communications, including text, photos, and files.
  • Scope: Affects all 450 million EU citizens; applies to end-to-end encrypted platforms.
  • Mechanism: Involves client-side scanning or other methods that weaken or bypass encryption, creating security vulnerabilities.
  • Legal Conflict: Challenges fundamental rights to privacy and data protection under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter.
  • Technical Issues: Automated scanning systems are prone to high rates of false positives, potentially flagging innocent content.
  • Precedent: Establishes a framework for mass surveillance that could be adopted by other governments globally.
  • Exemptions: EU politicians are excluded from this surveillance under "professional secrecy" rules.
