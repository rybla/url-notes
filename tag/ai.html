    • Output Limit: 128,000 tokens (includes "invisible" reasoning tokens).
    • Modalities: Supports text and image input; text-only output.

    • Improvements: Focused on reducing hallucinations, sycophancy, and improving instruction following, particularly for writing, coding, and health queries.

The Future of Art with AI - Econlib
aiarteconomicslaw of demandmarginal utilityimperfect substitutionmarket bifurcationlabor marketfuture of workautomationcreative industriescomparative advantage
  • Law of Demand: As AI lowers the price of creating art, the quantity of art demanded will increase.
  • Diminishing Marginal Utility: The increased demand will primarily serve low-marginal-benefit uses (e.g., personal jokes, blog post illustrations) that were previously not worth the cost of hiring an artist. This creates a new market rather than displacing jobs.
  • Imperfect Substitution: For high-marginal-use art (e.g., commercial products, custom portraits), AI is an imperfect substitute for human artists due to difficulties in communication, interpretation, and achieving specific, nuanced results.
  • Market Bifurcation: The art market is expected to split:
    • A low-marginal-use segment dominated by AI.
    • A high-marginal-use segment where human artists maintain a comparative advantage.
  • Job Impact: Job losses will be concentrated on "intramarginal" artists, whose work is most directly substitutable by AI's capabilities. The overall impact is not predicted to be an existential crisis for the art industry.
28 slightly rude notes on writing
writingcreativitythinkingphilosophy of writingartificial intelligenceaimeaningauthenticitycommunicationcraftartemotionlegacycontextsincerity
  • Motive and Cost: Effective writing requires a strong, intrinsic "motive." The writer must answer "Why do you care?" not just "Why should the reader care?" This act of writing is a costly, and therefore meaningful, signal of that care, a quality absent in zero-cost AI-generated text.
  • Thinking vs. Writing: Writing problems are fundamentally thinking problems. Clarity of expression cannot be achieved without clarity of thought. The goal is to operate in the liminal space between the obvious and the obviously wrong.
  • Context over Content: The meaning of text ("the necklace") is derived from its human context ("the neck")—the writer's specific circumstances, intent, and sacrifice. AI-generated prose, lacking this "coming-from-ness," is all necklace, no neck.
  • The Nature of Creation: The creative process is described as an unnatural and painful act of forcing precision from the mind. This pain can be addictive, making writing a compulsion for some.
  • Emotion and Sincerity: Bitterness is a sterile emotion for writing because it is self-absorbed. In contrast, writing from despair is an inherent act of hope, a subtextual plea for connection.
  • Legacy and Misinterpretation: A writer's legacy is often outside their control; they are frequently misunderstood or remembered for works they deemed secondary.
  • AI as a Catalyst: Competition from AI presents an opportunity for human writers to focus on their unique advantages: exploring the ineffable, personal experience and "widening the sphere of human sensibility."

Can AI Mediation Improve Democratic Deliberation?
aidemocracypoliticsdeliberationmediationai mediationlarge language modelsllmscomputational social sciencedeliberative democracyhabermas machineconsensus buildingcommon groundpolitical technologyai ethicsalgorithmic biasfairnessscalabilitysocial choice theorygroup decision-makinghuman-ai collaborationdeliberative trilemma
  • Core Problem: The paper addresses Fishkin's "deliberative trilemma"—the inherent tension between achieving broad participation, political equality, and meaningful deliberation simultaneously in democratic processes.
  • Proposed Solution: The use of AI-mediated deliberation, exemplified by the "Habermas Machine" (HM), an LLM-based system designed to find common ground among diverse viewpoints.
  • Habermas Machine (HM) Architecture:
    • Simulated Election: The HM generates a group statement by simulating an election.
    • Generative Model: Proposes a large set of candidate group statements based on participant inputs.
    • Personalized Reward Model (PRM): Predicts how each participant would rank the candidate statements.
    • Aggregation Mechanism: Uses a social choice function (e.g., Schulze method) to aggregate the predicted rankings and select a winning statement.
    • Iterative Process: Supports multiple rounds of participant critique and AI-driven revision to refine the outcome.
  • Key Findings (from Tessler, Bakker, et al., 2024):
    • The HM outperformed human mediators in generating common ground statements that participants endorsed.
    • The system's scalability and efficiency are its key advantages over human mediation.
    • Iterative rounds of critique and revision led to higher participant approval.
    • The process was found to be fair in representing majority/minority viewpoints, though it could overweight minority critiques in revision rounds.
  • Addressing the Trilemma:
    • Equality (Fairness): Fairness is addressed through architectural design (fair aggregation, ranking, and generation) and requires continuous monitoring with interpretability tools to prevent algorithmic bias.
    • Participation (Scalability): AI mediation can scale to large groups, but this creates a "scalable oversight" problem. Hierarchical aggregation (small groups deliberating, then aggregating group outputs) is proposed as a transparent and verifiable solution.
    • Deliberation (Quality): AI can enhance deliberative quality by acting as an "expert on tap," providing trustworthy information, or serving as a deliberative coach for individuals. This requires careful design to avoid manipulation and ensure AI factuality.
  • Limitations & Future Directions:
    • AI-mediated caucus deliberation lacks the social-relational dynamics (e.g., trust, empathy) of face-to-face interaction and must overcome potential "algorithmic aversion."
    • The system assumes good-faith participation; strategic misrepresentation of preferences is an open research problem.
    • Potential applications range from informal online forums (e.g., X's Community Notes) to formal processes like scaling deliberative polls and facilitating multilateral negotiations.
Why LLMs Can't Really Build Software - Zed Blog
llmsoftware engineeringmental modelsaiprogrammingai limitationsautonomous agentscognitionsoftware development
  • Core Thesis: LLMs cannot effectively build non-trivial software because they are incapable of creating, maintaining, and comparing the two distinct mental models essential for engineering: the model of the requirements and the model of what the code actually does.
  • Effective Software Engineering Loop:
    1. Build a mental model of requirements.
    2. Write code to implement the model.
    3. Build a mental model of the code's actual behavior.
    4. Identify discrepancies and iterate on code or requirements.
  • LLM Failures in the Loop:
    • LLMs cannot reliably perform step 3 or 4. They struggle to form an accurate model of the code they've written and get confused when tests fail, guessing whether to fix the code or the tests.
    • They lack the ability to "zoom" in and out of context levels or to temporarily stash a problem's context to solve a sub-problem, unlike human engineers.
  • Identified Technical Weaknesses:
    • Context Omission: Models fail to acquire relevant context that is not explicitly provided.
    • Recency Bias: Model outputs are disproportionately influenced by the most recent information in the context window.
    • Hallucination: Models generate plausible but incorrect details.
  • Conclusion: While useful for generating code for simple, well-defined tasks, LLMs currently function as a tool, not an autonomous engineer. The human developer remains responsible for driving the development process by managing the core mental models and verifying the output.

Rethinking CLI interfaces for AI
aillmcliapi designagent-based systemshuman-computer interactionsystem designinformation architecturedeveloper toolsai experienceaixshell scriptingsoftware engineering
  • Core Problem: Current CLI and API designs are inefficient for LLM agents, leading to excessive tool calls and context window overflow, particularly with local models.
  • API Design for Agents:
    • APIs should be designed to guide LLMs toward using high-level, convenient functions over low-level, verbose ones.
    • A temporary mitigation is embedding explicit guidance in docstrings to influence function selection.
  • Agent Interaction with CLIs:
    • Agents frequently fail by executing commands in the wrong directory or attempting to bypass quality control hooks (e.g., git commit --no-verify).
    • System-level enforcement (e.g., git wrappers blocking --no-verify, file permissions preventing hook modification) is necessary to keep agents compliant.
  • Proposed Solution: Information Architecture for LLMs:
    • Augment standard CLI tools to provide more contextual information to agents.
    • Example 1: Wrap head to cache output, structure it, and report the total number of lines, preventing resource-intensive re-execution.
    • Example 2: Enhance the command_not_found shell handler to report the current directory ($PWD) and suggest potential correct directories.
  • Future Direction:
    • Develop a suite of LLM-native CLI tools or a custom shell environment for agents.
    • Establish a new discipline, "AI Experience" (AIX), to formalize the principles of designing information systems for non-human users.

From GPT-2 to gpt-oss: Analyzing the Architectural Advances
large language modelllmmodel architecturegpt-ossgpt-2mixture-of-expertsmoermsnormswiglurotary position embeddingsropegrouped query attentiongqasliding-window attentionquantizationreinforcement learningopen sourceapache 2.0aideep learningtechnical analysisgpu

  • Architectural Evolution from GPT-2:

    • Normalization: Replaced LayerNorm with the computationally simpler RMSNorm.
    • Activation: Switched from GELU to a gated SwiGLU (Swish Gated Linear Unit), offering better expressivity with fewer parameters.
    • Positional Encoding: Upgraded from learned absolute positional embeddings to Rotary Position Embeddings (RoPE).
    • Attention:
      • Uses Grouped Query Attention (GQA) for improved efficiency over Multi-Head Attention (MHA).
      • Implements sliding-window attention (128-token window) in every other layer, a technique reportedly used in GPT-3.
      • Re-introduces bias units in attention projections and adds learned "attention sinks" (per-head bias logits) to stabilize attention over long contexts.
    • Regularization: Removed dropout, which is common in modern LLMs trained on massive datasets for a single epoch.

  • Core Architecture:

    • Mixture-of-Experts (MoE): Replaces standard feed-forward networks with MoE layers. The gpt-oss-20b model uses 32 experts and activates 4 per token.
    • Width vs. Depth: Compared to the contemporary Qwen3 model, gpt-oss is a "wider" architecture (larger embedding dimension) rather than "deeper" (fewer transformer blocks).

  • Training and Features:

    • Reasoning Control: Trained with a high-compute reinforcement learning stage that allows users to control reasoning verbosity and accuracy at inference time via a system prompt (Reasoning effort: low/medium/high).
    • Quantization: Released with an MXFP4 quantization scheme for MoE experts, enabling the 120B model to run on a single 80GB H100 GPU and the 20B model on a 16GB RTX 50-series GPU.
    • License: Apache 2.0 open-weight license.
