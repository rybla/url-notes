*Saving Can-Do* - Marginal REVOLUTION
politicslaweconomicsus politicsgovernancepolicybureaucracyregulationderegulationinfrastructurelaw reformlegal reformadministrative lawenvironmental lawnepajudicial reviewexecutive powergovernment efficiency
  • Problem: The American "can-do" spirit is hindered by bureaucratic and legal obstacles, requiring systemic reform to restore efficient project execution and decision-making.
  • Proposed Solution: A three-pronged approach involving a new legal framework, a new trust-building institution, and a special commission to design the implementation details.
  • Legal Framework Reforms:
    • Centralized Authority: Designate a single lead agency for each infrastructure category, with ultimate approval authority subject to White House oversight. Federal decisions would preempt state/local approvals for nationally significant projects.
    • Goal-Oriented Regulation: Recodify 50 years of accumulated mandates into a set of public goals that allow for balancing and trade-offs, rather than rigid, prescriptive rules.
    • Executive Override: Grant the executive branch interim authority to approve permits "notwithstanding provisions of law to the contrary," provided a public interest justification is given.
    • Streamlined Process: Redefine procedural laws like NEPA to focus on transparency and public comment, with judicial review limited to an "abuse of discretion" standard, not strict procedural compliance.
    • Limited Judicial Review: Curtail court jurisdiction to prevent lawsuits based on procedural inadequacy. Legal challenges should be restricted to actions that exceed the executive's defined authority.

  • Child Protective Services (CPS) Overreach: A case study where CPS investigated a mother for allowing unsupervised outdoor play, citing internal rules that exceed Virginia state law by requiring parents to be "visible to... neighbors" regardless of whether the children are visible to the parent.
  • Guaranteed Annual Income (GAI) Study: An NBER working paper details a randomized controlled trial where a group receiving $1,000/month for three years saw a reduction in work equivalent to ~8 days/year. While household income increased by ~$6,100, this was driven by the transfer payment itself.
  • Critique of Economic Myths: A review of "The Triumph of Economic Freedom" by Gramm and Boudreaux, which uses historical data to debunk common economic fallacies (e.g., that the New Deal ended the Great Depression or that free trade impoverished America). The book highlights significant global welfare gains from 1980-2000, such as a 43% decline in malnutrition death rates and a six-year increase in global life expectancy, concurrent with population growth.
  • Tariff Uniformity: An economic thought experiment by Alex Tabarrok demonstrates that a uniform tariff on imports from multiple countries (e.g., a 10% tariff on cars from both Japan and Korea) can be welfare-improving compared to a selective tariff on just one country, assuming the goods are perfect substitutes. The uniformity of the tariff is a critical assumption.

What the Columbia Settlement Really Means
columbia universitytrump administrationlawhigher educationsettlementanti-discrimination lawfederal grantsuniversity fundingacademic freedompoliticsuniversity-government relations

After months of negotiation, Columbia University announced on July 23 that it had reached an agreement with the Trump administration to resolve investigations into alleged violations of federal anti-discrimination laws. The settlement provides that Columbia will pay fines of $221m over three years, and that in consideration for these payments and other concessions—including concessions made by Columbia as a precondition to the negotiations —the Trump administration will reinstate the “vast majority” of the federal grants the government paused or canceled in March. In announcing the settlement, Columbia stated that the settlement’s terms are “carefully crafted to protect the values that define us.” In Columbia’s telling, the settlement “preserves Columbia’s autonomy and authority over faculty hiring, admissions, and academic decision-making.”

  • Proposal: EU "Chat Control" mandates automated scanning of all private digital communications, including text, photos, and files.
  • Scope: Affects all 450 million EU citizens; applies to end-to-end encrypted platforms.
  • Mechanism: Involves client-side scanning or other methods that weaken or bypass encryption, creating security vulnerabilities.
  • Legal Conflict: Challenges fundamental rights to privacy and data protection under Articles 7 and 8 of the EU Charter.
  • Technical Issues: Automated scanning systems are prone to high rates of false positives, potentially flagging innocent content.
  • Precedent: Establishes a framework for mass surveillance that could be adopted by other governments globally.
  • Exemptions: EU politicians are excluded from this surveillance under "professional secrecy" rules.
Wikimedia Foundation Challenges UK Online Safety Act Regulations
wikipediawikimedia foundationukonline safety actofcomregulationlawlegalidentity verificationprivacysafetyhuman rightslegal challenge
  • Subject: Wikimedia Foundation's legal challenge to the UK's Online Safety Act (OSA) Categorisation Regulations.
  • Core Issue: The regulations risk assigning Wikipedia "Category 1" status, imposing the most stringent OSA duties, which include mandatory identity verification for many contributors.
  • Claimant's Argument:
    • Category 1 duties are designed for high-risk commercial platforms, not non-profit, volunteer-moderated projects like Wikipedia.
    • Identity verification would undermine contributor privacy and safety, potentially exposing them to harassment, lawsuits, or state-level persecution.
    • Compliance would divert critical resources and expose the platform to increased vandalism and manipulation.
  • Legal Status & Outcome:
    • The High Court of Justice heard the case on July 22-23, 2025.
    • On August 11, 2025, the court dismissed the Foundation's challenge.
  • Judicial Guidance:
    • The ruling is not a "green light" for the regulator (Ofcom) to implement the OSA in a way that harms Wikipedia.
    • The court acknowledged Wikipedia's value and the potential human rights impact on its volunteers.
    • The judge suggested Ofcom may require a "particularly flexible interpretation" of the rules, or that parliamentary amendment may be necessary to protect Wikipedia.
  • Next Step: First categorization decisions by Ofcom are expected in Summer 2025.
Wikipedia loses challenge against Online Safety Act verification rules
wikipediaonline safety actuklawlegalregulationprivacyidentity verification
  • Legal Action: The Wikimedia Foundation lost a judicial review challenging the UK's Online Safety Act regulations.
  • Core Conflict: Wikimedia argued that the act's "Category 1" classification, intended for large social media sites, was too broad and improperly applied to Wikipedia.
  • Primary Concern: If classified as "Category 1," Wikipedia might be forced to implement identity verification for its contributors, which it claims would undermine the privacy and safety of its volunteer editors.
  • Court's Ruling: The High Court dismissed the challenge to the lawfulness of the government's decision-making process for the regulations.
  • Future Implications: The judgment leaves open the possibility for future legal challenges, specifically against Ofcom's potential decision to classify Wikipedia as "Category 1" or if the act's implementation makes the site's operation unviable.
