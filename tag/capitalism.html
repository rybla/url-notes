Today's Convergence of Political Systems - Econlib
political scienceeconomicshistoryphilosophycapitalismstate capitalismcommunismsocialismpolitical economyconvergence theoryindividualismcollectivismeconomic growthstate controlus politicschinese economy
  • Historical Convergence (1960s-70s): The dominant belief was a convergence between US-style capitalism and Soviet-style communism. The author reframes this as a broader trend: the supremacy of collective, political choice subsuming the philosophy of individual, private choice.
  • Modern Convergence (Present): This trend has accelerated, with the US increasingly adopting "state capitalism." This system is a hybrid, distinct from socialism (state ownership of production), where the state guides and directs nominally private enterprises, mirroring aspects of China's economic model.
  • Efficacy of State Control: The author, citing analysis of China's economy, argues that real economic growth originates from market-based sources, not state direction, suggesting the primacy of collective choice is an illusion.
Modern Culture Trends
sociologyculturemodernitysocial changeeconomicstechnologypoliticspsychologyindividualismsecularismrationalismegalitarianismcapitalismconsumerismglobalizationurbanizationsocial normssignaling theoryartmediafamilygenderworkeducation

  • Learning & Information:

    • Decreased belief in supernatural phenomena; increased trust in science and experts.
    • Shift in purity rituals to focus on germ theory.
    • Increased focus on psychological interiors in fiction and morality.

  • Economic & Technological Development:

    • Adoption of precise timekeeping, measurement, and naming systems.
    • Shift from self-production to a consumer economy with increased wage labor.
    • Increased population density, mobility, and long-distance communication, weakening local and kin-based ties.
    • Growth of larger, for-profit organizations and more capable, regulatory governments.
    • Formal education displacing child labor, fostering abstract and analytical thinking.
    • Market and state insurance displacing informal risk-sharing networks.

  • Egalitarianism:

    • Rejection of social hierarchies based on inherited or group characteristics (family, gender, race, etc.).
    • Repudiation of slavery, colonialism, and aristocracy in favor of democracy.
    • Increased sympathy for rebellion against authority.

  • Effort Minimization (Lazy/Selfish/Myopic):

    • Reduced work, delayed marriage, and fewer children.
    • Deregulation of personal expression and social interaction norms.
    • De-emphasis on self-control; increased acceptance of promiscuity and recreational drugs.
    • Prioritization of individual comfort and happiness over community duty and honor.

  • Signaling:

    • Cost-ineffective behaviors to signal positive traits (e.g., over-insurance to show care, over-consumption to show wealth, excessive schooling to show conscientiousness).
    • Following fashion and news to signal social connection.
    • Seeking novelty and variety to signal authenticity and creativity.

  • Non-adaptive Enjoyment:

    • Mate selection based on personal choice rather than arrangement.
    • Increased consumption of fiction and music.
    • Over-eating and expression of more gendered personalities.
    • Wealth-driven paternalism and cultural activism as a form of status-seeking.
